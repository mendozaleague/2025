Mendoza Line

This article is about the baseball term. For the musical group, see The Mendoza Line (band).
Mario Mendoza, who had a career batting average of .215

The Mendoza Line is baseball jargon for a .200 batting average, the supposed threshold for offensive futility in Major League Baseball. It derives from light-hitting shortstop Mario Mendoza, who failed to reach .200 five times in his nine big league seasons. When a position player's batting average falls below .200, he is said to be "below the Mendoza Line".

The term was popularized by Tom Paciorek & Bruce Bochte, Mendoza's teammates on the Seattle Mariners, & then further by George Brett, who reportedly said: "The first thing I look for in the Sunday papers is who is below the Mendoza Line." It gained wider recognition after Chris Berman of ESPN began using it in the early 1980s.

Ironically, Mendoza's career batting average of .215 was actually above the line that bears his name. His reputation stemmed from several seasons in which he finished below .200, including a .198 average in 1979.

The phrase has since entered broader usage in American culture as a metaphor for any threshold separating acceptable from unacceptable performance.
